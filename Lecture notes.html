Note 2 to entry: The system environment includes external entities that can have various influences upon the system-of-interest, such as developmental, technological, business, operational, organizational, political, economic, legal, regulatory, ecological and social influences as well as external physical effects such as electromagnetic radiation, charged particles, gravitational effects, or electric and magnetic fields.

Note 3 to entry: A system function depends on the boundary between the system-of-interest and the system environment.

A walk through the fundamentals of systems engineering

This section is a pragmatic journey through the systems engineering landscape. Although the encoding of Systems Engineering concepts often relies on models created with a modelling notation/language and software that help the members of a project team to communicate, in this section, the illustrations of concepts will voluntary introduce alternative means to communicate design information rather than imposing a "universal" solution.

What kind of system are we talking about?

A system is often defined as the combination of interacting elements. In this course, we focus on an artificial (i.e. man-made, technical, technological) system. Consequently, natural systems such as the human body are out of scope. In practice, the interpretation of meaning is frequently clarified by the use of an associative noun, e.g., autoinjector system or helicopter system.

A technical system is a combination of interacting elements organized to satisfy one or more system requirements. Thus, a technical system has a purpose. It is created and utilised to provide services, in a defined operating environment (or context), for the benefit of users and other stakeholders. A system is comprised of multiple systems usually referred as subsystems or system elements. Subsystems have relationships between each other and can be of different nature: people, hardware, software, facilities, policies, processes and documents. The order and arrangement of the subsystems affect the system performances. Finally, a system has a boundary that separates it from external systems and stakeholders (see illustration above).

Technical systems are becoming more and more complex. We moved from the era of craftsman who used creativity to offer highly customised - not to say unique - products to their customers to standardized industrial systems based on repeatable processes. After the era of greatest inventions, which aimed at solving technical problems, we observe that complex technical systems have been invented based on the combination of greatest inventions. For instance, the transportation system is a network of cars, the communication system is a network of telephones, and so on. More recently, complex systems have been integrated to give birth to socio-technical systems of systems by merging. For instance the electrical cars network is a system of systems that integrates the transportation system and the energy system. The growing systemic complexity can be summarised by four dimensions:

  • The technical complexity (growing number of parts and interfaces, multi-physical phenomenon, emergent properties, etc.)

  • The diversity (product lines which require to manage several variants and options)

  • The organisational complexity (extended enterprise, multi-site, multi-national, etc.)

  • The prescriptive texts (laws, standards, internal and external policies, guidelines, etc.)

Among the numerous characteristics of complex systems, emergent properties is maybe what best characterise the systemic complexity. An emergent property is a new property which is found at higher levels of a system as a result of interactions at a lower level. Traffic is a familiar example of emergent properties — it’s a property of a collection of cars but not of an individual car. In the realm of physics, things like temperature and pressure are emergent; an individual molecule doesn’t have a temperature or pressure, which are properties of collections of molecules like gases or liquids. Causation does not only point from lower to higher systemic levels; the properties emerging from collective behaviour can also constrain the behaviour of the individual entities. Again, traffic serves as an excellent example: while traffic emerges from the interaction of many individual cars, it also affects their behaviour, limiting and guiding them.

The nature of systems engineering: is it an approach, process, [...], method?

Whether it is in industry or academic research, Systems Engineering (SE) is defined as an approach, a process or a method for engineering technical systems.

A process is a set of activities that tells you "What" to do, "When" to deliver it, and "Who" is responsible for it. Below are some examples of systems engineering or, more generally, new product development processes.

A method tells you “How” to practically perform the activities in order to get the expected results (e.g. How to capture needs? How to write good requirements? How to create modular architectures?). A method often comes with its own limited “process”. You will not find any method that covers all activities of your design process but someone will have to work-out methodological elements to reliably perform them. There is no clear boundary between sub-processes and methods. For instance, in the 6-activity (Emphasize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test, Assess) Design Thinking process illustrated below, the bullet points are sub-activities that tells you « How » to perform the main activities. In this course, we will consider the set of sub-activities (e.g., Conduct interviews, Uncover emotions, Seek stories) as a sub-process and not a method as there is no clear procedure to perform the activity Empathize.

However, the Design Thinking Toolbox offers a clear definition of the Design Thinking process and method. Indeed, the Design Thinking Toolbox propose a 7-activity process (Understand, Observe, Define point of view, Ideate, Prototype, Test, Reflect).

Note that this Design Thinking process slightly differs from the previous one. Thus, we have one approach know as "Design Thinking" and already two processes, a 6-activity process and a 7-activity. This is the reason why - in this course - we can discuss about the Design Thinking approach and not the Design Thinking process, but we may discuss the 7-activity design thinking process proposed in the Design Thinking Toolbox. In addition to the process, the Design Thinking Toolbox proposes a set of methods to support the practitioners. Getting back to Systems Engineering, it cannot be a method or a process as there is no single recipe to do it. Each company has its own set of processes and methods for implementing Systems Engineering. Consequently, Systems Engineering is a design approach. In this course, a design approach is a logical way of thinking that relies upon several elements - convictions, processes, methods, tools... - establishing a design discipline among New Product Development (NPD) team members. Systems Engineering is a methodical and interdisciplinary design approach that mainly concentrates on four main activities: requirements definition, requirements validation and architecture design performed recursively in a top-down process, plus integration and verification of design and implementation performed recursively in a bottom-up process.

Systems engineering is a methodical, disciplined approach for the design, realization, technical management, operations, and retirement of a system. […] It is a way of looking at the “big picture” when making technical decisions. It is a way of achieving stakeholder functional, physical, and operational performance requirements in the intended use environment over the planned life of the systems. In other words, systems engineering is a logical way of thinking. (NASA, 2007)

Systems engineering is an interdisciplinary approach and means to enable the realization of successful systems. It focuses on defining customer needs and required functionality early in the development cycle, documenting requirements, and then proceeding with design synthesis and system validation while considering the complete problem: operations, cost and schedule, performance, training and support, test, manufacturing, and disposal. Systems engineering integrates all the disciplines and specialty groups into a team effort forming a structured development process that proceeds from concept to production to operation. Systems engineering considers both the business and the technical needs of all customers with the goal of providing a quality product that meets the user needs. (INCOSE, 2004)

The adoption of a design approach is mainly a matter of convictions

Note that convictions play an important role in the adoption (e.g., by the top management of a company or targeted practitioners) of a design approach. Indeed, the reputation of the organisation (company or research lab) in which the approach has been pioneered serves as a proxy for justifying the choice of one design approach over another. Looking for successful applications in well-known companies published by well-known academics explains how the uptake of a design approach can behave like fashions, where many companies take up a design approach at the same time and then also discard it at the same time.

The genesis of systems engineering

The Systems Engineering body of knowledge started to emerge during the world war II and the cold war with the development of missiles and missile-defense systems by the Department Of Defense of the USA. The main motivation was to work out a set of best practices to better manage the quality and costs of new complex military systems. Then, the NASA continued to improve the Systems Engineering approach, especially during the Apollo program, and published the NASA Systems Engineering Handbook. [Learn more]

 

Systems engineering is not the salvation of new product development

Systems Engineering is not the unique approach to develop engineered systems. For instance, the Design Thinking approach, which is also mistakenly considered as a design process or method, can also serve the same purpose, but each approach has its own characteristics.

Design Thinking often starts with human-centric qualitative needs, which are clarified by a Diverge & Converge process where ideation, prototyping and testing phases are conducted iteratively in order to clarify and satisfy the needs of the end-user. Design Thinking is often preferred for developing small-scale projects where there is room for creativity. Conversely, Systems Engineering requires to translate system functions into unambiguous quantitative system requirements an to systematically decompose high level requirements/designs into lower level requirements/design before continuously verifying and validating the integrated design from subsystems to system. Applying Systems Engineering to develop a bedside lamp may be of little interest since the very low level of complexity of the system does not require a decomposition into subsystems to be managed. Developing a new commercial aircraft with a Design Thinking approach starting from fuzzy needs, quick prototyping efforts and end-users testings is also not appropriate for such a complex system. However, both approaches can be mixed. In a Systems Engineering approach, Design Thinking can help to clarify qualitative requirements using formative studies which consist in creating a low-fidelity prototype and performing tests with targeted end-users. Design Thinking is also of interest to generate conceptual solutions to a given set of (system) requirements.

Systems engineering is an approach standardised by international organisations

Compared to many other design approaches, Systems Engineering is standardised by international standard development organisations (e.g., ISO, IEEE...) and is tailored to industries by more specific recommended practices (e.g., ARP in the aerospace industry, FDA in the food and drug industry...). Systems Engineering standards such as ISO 15288:2015 Systems and software engineering - System life cycle processes are key resources (sometimes mandatory) to perform systems engineering activities. Looking at the set of Systems Engineering processes defined in the ISO 15288:2015, you will notice that Systems Engineering is not all about technical processes but also enterprise, project, and agreement processes. Although it is relevant to consider both the the product "le système à faire" and the project "le système pour faire (le système à faire)", systems engineering is not about project management. In a company, it is a recommended to separate the systems engineer/architect role from the project manager role. In this course, we will concentrate on the core activities of the technical processes.

Three fundamental concepts of systems engineering

Systems engineering differs from other design approaches on different points, but there are three core concepts that make it original when they are methodically combined together.

Fundamental 1: System decomposition

As briefly discussed earlier, in the systems engineering approach, a technical system is decomposed into subsystems. Therefore, systems engineering is a top-down design approach largely inspired by the reductionism, a philosophical idea that asserts that entities of a given kind are identical to, or are collections or combinations of, entities of another - often simpler - kind.

A subsystem (also know as system element) is a combination of interacting elements that constitute a system and are organized to satisfy a set of subsystem requirements and contribute to the realization of higher-level system or subsystem requirements to which the subsystem belongs, but that does not, on its own, satisfy them. A subsystem is a system in its own right. Each layer of a system decomposition is a systemic level.

The decomposition of a system into subsystems is usually performed up to a make, buy or reuse decision. In addition, as illustrated below, when decomposing a system into subsystem, it is necessary to make sure that the external interfaces - of a system or subsystem - are conserved.

Fundamental 2: Problem space VS. Solution space

Systems engineering is a top-down causal approach for problem solving. Thus, for a given system or subsystem, we successively work out two spaces : the problem space (the specification) followed by the solution space (the design).

The definition of the problem space or specification requires to adopt a "black box" or "external" view on the system, whereas the definition of the solution space or design requires to adopt a "white box" or "internal" view on the system.

Depending on the system-of-interest, that is, the system whose life cycle is under consideration, we can adopt a black box/internal view or a "white box/external view. For instance, in the example below, if the system-of-interest is the air transportation system, then the black box/external view includes the ground transportation system and the maritime transport system, whereas the white box/internal view includes the ticketing system, air traffic control system, airports system, fuel distribution system, and aircraft system. However, if the system of interest is the aircraft system, then the black box/external view includes the ticketing system, air traffic control system, airports system and fuel distribution system, whereas the white box/internal view includes the airframe system, life support system, navigation system, flight control system, air crew, and propulsion system.

Fundamental 3: Zigzagging between the problem space and the solution space

As we decompose the system into lower levels of abstraction, each system or subsystem within each level of abstraction represents a Problem Space that requires one or more lower-level Solution Spaces.

The top-down decomposition follows a zigzagging pattern between the problem space and the solutions space. After defining the system requirements we have to "zig" to the design of the system architecture, that is, the decomposition of the system into subsystems and the definition of internal interfaces between the subsystems. After a proper system architecture design, we have to "zag" to the subsystems requirements for further decompostion.

In some large organisations, there exists a “division for engineering requirements” at all systemic levels by remaining only in the problem space. The Function analysis system technique (FAST) and Structured Analysis and Design Technique (SADT) methods are good examples of such techniques. As should be quite obvious by now, these divisions cannot do their job right, since requirements cannot be decomposed by remaining in one domain, that is, without zigzagging. Thus, when designers are forced to work in such an organisation, they often develop requirements by thinking of an already existing design, which results in re-specifying that which already exists. Moreover, they will not get the results they are looking for and miss important opportunities for innovation.

Systems engineering processes: focus on specification and architecture definition

Systems Engineering approach is well-known for its V-cycle (aka. V-model) new product development process. A more realistic representation of a (and not THE!) Systems Engineering-based new product development process is the Double Vee process where each element at a given systemic level follows its own V-cycle process.

A review of popular Systems Engineering references shows that the activities of a Systems Engineering process are performed recursively from top to down, that is, from system to subsystems, and iteratively, that is, going back and forth between specification and design, for a give system-of-interest.

Greenfield/Clean sheet systems VS. Brownfield/Legacy systems

“Greenfield” and “brownfield” are terms used in real estate. Greenfield land is previously undeveloped space, such as a (green) farmer’s field. Brownfield land has been previously developed, typically has existing structures and services in place, and may contain undesirable or hazardous materials (also known as waste) that must be remediated.

Greenfield systems engineering, also known as “clean sheet” or “blank slate” systems engineering, involves systems that are new designs and have no, or limited, legacy systems constraints, other than system interfaces. Traditionally, systems engineering has been taught by considering systems from a greenfield perspective. One starts with a “clean or blank sheet of paper” and determines the set of stakeholders and their needs, translates them into system requirements and validates them, architects and designs a system solution, implements the system elements, and then continuously integrates and verifies subsystems' design and implementation. While this is an effective way to teach systems engineering and to prepare practitioners with skills applicable to the entire system life cycle, few system development efforts are truly greenfield. Greenfield, therefore, is an almost theoretical situation that is rarely seen in practice. Sometimes, it can be quite traumatic for organizations that make brownfield updates to its legacy products over a long period of time to transition from brownfield development back to greenfield. They may need to “relearn” how to do greenfield.

Brownfield systems engineering, also known as “legacy” SE, involves significant modifications, extensions, or replacement of an existing “as-is” system in an existing environment to an updated “to-be” system. Brownfield systems often contain waste (e.g., technical debt) that may need to be remediated. “In-service” systems are another example of brownfield system. Brownfield systems typically have explicit continuity requirements, where the operation of the as-is system needs to continue, resulting in a deliberate transition to the updated system.

The nature of greenfield and brownfield systems drives different life cycle approaches that reflect different areas of emphasis. This impacts not only the system solution, but also the team that is put in place to develop the system. As with all development efforts, systems engineering processes need to be tailored to fit the needs of a given project.

Our systems engineering process

One reason so many design mistakes are being made today is that design is being done empirically on a trial-and-error basis. This problem is not confined to any one country or any one company. It exists everywhere. Universities throughout the world have not given their engineering students generalized, codified, and systematic knowledge in design. Instead, design has been treated as a subject that is not amenable to scientific treatment. Consequently, design has depended on intuitive and innate reasoning rather than on rigorous scientific study. This section will introduce some good practices to develop engineered systems and reduce the intuitive trial-and-error approach.

Develop system requirements

Often designers find that the precise description of "what we want to achieve" is a difficult task. Many designers deliberately leave them implicit rather than explicit and then, start working on design solutions even before they have clearly defined their design goals. They measure their success by comparing their design with the implicit design goals they had in mind. They spend a great deal of time to improve and iterate the design until the design solution and "what they had in mind" converge, which is a time consuming process at best. To be efficient and to generate the design that meets the perceived needs, we must specifically state the design goals in terms of "what we want to achieve" and begin the design process. Iterations between "what" and "how" are necessary, but each iteration loop must redefine "what" clearly.

Define system functions

The specification of a system usually starts by the definition of needs. A need is an agreed-to expectation for a system to perform a system function or satisfy a design constraint imposed on the system.

A system function is an effect – intended by a stakeholder – of the interaction of the system with the system context. It is a unitless term that does not express a level of performance to be achieved.

A system function is a function whose effects can be observed from outside the system and depends on the boundary between the system-of-interest and the system environment. A system function can bee seen as a service provided by the system to a stakeholder.

  • Problem statement

A design challenge starts with an understanding of the problem/mission statement. However, before we begin to solve a problem, we must first understand it correctly. Develop a common understanding of a problem with the clients and the team. The problem/mission statement is a simple sentence that is neither too specific (solution agnostic) nor too generic (5W: what, why, who, where, when).

  • Why the purely functional techniques - e.g., APTE, FAST, [...], SADT - are not enough?

As exemplified in the paper "Systemic Functional Analysis Method for Design Retrieval and Documentation", in academia - and especially in France in my opinion -, to specify system requirements we are used to teach purely functional techniques. By purely functional techniques, I mean an analysis that consists in breaking down high-level functions into more detailed functions without zigzagging, i.e., by remaining only in the specification/problem domain. There are two major reasons to argue that such techniques are unsuitable for systems which are not toy systems.

The first reason is that a function is not a requirement. Indeed a function is unitless, it does not prescribe any performance value. Consequently, one cannot choose or design an appropriate solution that solves the problem by satisfying the requirements. In the example below, the function Capter les ordres de la télécommande can be realized by many designs, such as a Bluetooth receptor

The second reason is that to decompose functions (and requirements too), the designer must zigzag. For example, suppose you want to design a vehicle that satisfies the following four system functions: go forward, go backward, stop, and turn. We cannot decompose these system functions into lower level technical functions unless we first conceptualize the design solution that can satisfy these highest-level functions. If we decide to use an electric motor as a design concept to realize the functions of moving forward, the decomposed technical functions at the next systemic level - i.e., the motor level - would be quite different from those that would have resulted had we chosen gas turbines as the design concept. Therefore, when we define the functions in a "solution-neutral" environment, we have to "zig" to the solution/design domain, and after proper design concepts are chosen, we have to "zag" to the problem/specification domain for further decomposition.

The design choices made at a given systemic level will influence the functions and requirements at the next systemic level. For instance, consider the following design problem. The pilot of the helicopter system desires to hoist a load. Based on two different operational scenarios, the system function "to hoist a load" is derived into two system requirements. The first operational scenario for mountain rescue operations consists in hoisting a person and a gendarme. Based on this operational scenario a first requirement "To hoist a load between 50 kg and 300 kg" is specified. A second operational scenario for delivery operations consists in hoisting an heavy load for construction work. A second requirement "To hoist a load between 100 kg and 800 kg" is specified.

The architecture design at the helicopter system level includes several subsystems including a structure (or fuselage) subsystem, a landing gear subsystem, an avionic subsystem, an electric hoisting subsystem, and an electric generation and distribution subsystem. At this stage, an electric hoisting subsystem has been preferred but another alternative could be a manual hoisting subsystem.

Because of the electric hoisting subsystem, in the hoisting subsystem specification document (or model), there will be a new subsystem function (and requirement) "To be electrically powered" which would not be needed if the hoisting subsystem was manual. A purely functional technique would increase the chance to overlook some requirements.

 

  • Needs elicitation techniques

Starting from a problem/mission statement that is communicated in a single thought sentence, a set of needs can be elaborated (decomposed and derived). Depending on the system to develop and the design context, needs can be identified with different techniques. The most common approach is to interview internal and external stakeholders and ask them what they expect from the system. A similar qualitative approach named focus group consists of bringing together a small group of stakeholders to answer questions in a moderated setting. Marketing survey - online or not - is also an option to elucidate needs, but it does not offer the opportunity to directly engage with customers so as to better understand their problems, desires, wishes, expectations, etc. When developing consumer products, the benchmarking of competing products or formative user studies may be of interest to identify core system functions and expected performances.

  • System life cycle definition

Whatever your preferred elicitation techniques, to maximize the level of confidence in the completeness of the set of needs, you can perform a system analysis based on the following assumptions. If you are exhaustive in the identification of external entities (stakeholders and external systems) and environmental conditions, then you will increase the chance of omitting any external entities. To make sure that the collection of external entities is exhaustive, you can analyse all contexts for each phase of the system life cycle.

The definition of a system life cycle consists of the identification of the set of phases during which activities are performed that enable the achievement of objectives for that period of time. Life cycle phases could be procurement, development, test, verification, validation, manufacturing, transportation, deployment, installation, training, operations, logistics, maintenance, upgrades, or disposal. Often there is a distinct set of stakeholders and interfaces associated with each life cycle phase. Each has unique needs and requirements. Not addressing a life cycle phase could result in omitting needs and requirements.

  • External entities

  • Services

Needs, use cases, user stories, functions, services, intended effects, [...], constraints

The definition of services consists in adopting an external “Black-box” view on the system of interest (SoI) and to specify the effects – intended by its stakeholders – of the interaction of the SoI with its operating environment.

For each lifecycle stage, ask the stakeholders to describe a “day-in-the life” of the SOI. This could include multiple use cases or scenarios. Address both nominal and alternate nominal cases. For use cases, capture the initial conditions and state of the SOI at the end of the use case.

 

In addition to the nominal and alternate nominal scenarios, also ask the stakeholder about off-nominal scenarios- what could go wrong, or currently what often goes wrong? (The off-nominal scenarios will be a major source of risk -> Failure Modes and Effects Analysis (FMEA)).

 

The resulting use cases or scenarios provide key insights into the stakeholder needs and stakeholder-owned system requirements concerning features, capabilities, functionality, performance, interaction with other systems, standards, regulations, physical attributes, etc.

 

The goal of elicitation is to provide an implementation-free understanding of the stakeholders’ needs by defining what is expected (specification) without addressing how (design) to satisfy the set of needs.

 

  • Set need priority

It is common for a specific stakeholder to consider some aspects of the SOI more important than others. During elicitation activities, it is important to ask the stakeholders to prioritize what they are asking for. Some things will be especially important to the stakeholder, while other things may be “nice-to-haves” or “desires”, but not critical to the system being able to achieve the agreed to mission. There will be some things that the stakeholder may be able to “live without” given budget or schedule constraints or conflicting stakeholder needs and stakeholder-owned requirements.

Stakeholders need to make clear which things are critical for the system to be able to meet its intended purpose in the operating environment. When there is a difference in opinion as to priority or criticality, the ranking of the stakeholder must be considered.

When recording stakeholder inputs, it is important to record within the SOI’s integrated dataset the priority of their needs and the rationale concerning the designation.

Need rationale

For each need elicited from the stakeholders, it is important to capture the rationale concerning “why”. Rationale helps understand intent. To understand the real need, stakeholders may have to be asked “why?” multiple times.

  • If the need or requirement is based on an assumption, that assumption should be included in the rationale attribute.

  • If the need includes a number, the rationale should also include a description from which that number was derived.

  • If a stakeholder cannot provide a rationale, why include the need or requirement in the set?

  • Considerations for needs definition:

    • [Ambguities] The elicited needs and requirements will frequently include ambiguous terms and phrases like user friendly, robust, easy to use, works fast, safe, affordable, pleasant, easy to test, cost-effective manufacturing, works just like the last one only better, etc. It is common for stakeholders to state their needs at a high level of abstraction resulting in ambiguity as to their exact intent as it applies to the SOI.

    • [Implementation free] Stakeholders often state their needs as implementation statements or solutions rather than address the problem and needs concerning a solution to the problem. The focus should be on the "what" not "how”. When a stakeholder states a specific implementation or solution, ask “Why?” and “What does that implementation or solution allow you to do?” A common approach is to ask “why” multiple times. The answers to the questions will help uncover the real needs. In addition, avoiding implementation allows the project team to be more innovative in defining an effective design solution.

    • Stakeholders will often have both explicit AND implicit needs. Stakeholders will often focus on functionality, performance, and user interfaces, assuming everything else will be addressed by the developing project team. The stakeholders assume the project team knows what they are assuming. The stakeholder’s expectations for their implicit needs are often the same as for what they have explicitly stated. If the implicit needs are not met, the system may fail system validation, even though those needs and requirements were not explicitly stated. To avoid this issue, the project team must ask questions concerning areas of interest not explicitly stated by the stakeholders. For example, what quality attributes do they need the SOI to have? What standards or regulations need to be adhered to? What are the drivers and constraints?

    • Stories, scenarios, use cases, system concepts, ops concepts, concepts of operation, etc. often focus on functionality, performance, and interactions with other systems, but are often incomplete, not addressing other “non-functional” needs such as quality needs (-ilities), e.g. maintenability, availability, security, reliability…, design and construction standards, regulations, and physical characteristics. These other needs must also be included - otherwise the integrated set of needs and resulting set of design input requirements will be incomplete.

    • Drivers and constraints

      • Design constraints (parts, materials, organizational design best practices, etc.).

      • Design standards (industry, domain, business management, business operations).

      • Production constraints (existing technology, facilities, equipment, cost, throughput, etc.).

    • Human factors (human/machine interface - HMI).

      • Regulations (law).

    • Operating environment (natural, induced).

      • Operating environment (social, cultural).

    • Existing systems: (interactions, interfaces, dependencies).

System Modeling Language (SysML)

SysML Diagrams

 

  • Requirement diagram represents text-based requirements and their relationship with other requirements, design elements, and test cases to support requirements traceability (not in UML)

  • Activity diagram represents behavior in terms of the order in which actions execute based on the availability of their inputs, outputs, and control, and how the actions transform the inputs to outputs (modification of UML)

  • Sequence diagram represents behavior in terms of a sequence of messages exchanged between systems, or between parts of systems (same as UML )

  • State machine diagram represents behavior of an entity in terms of its transitions between states triggered by events (same as UML)

  • Use case diagram represents functionality in terms of how a system is used by external entities (i.e., actors) to accomplish a set of goals (same as UML)

  • Block definition diagram represents structural elements called blocks, and their composition and classification (modification of UML)

  • Internal block diagram represents interconnection and interfaces between the parts of a block (modification of UML composite structure diagram)

  • Parametric diagram represents constraints on property values, such as F [ m * a, used to support engineering analysis (not in UML)

SysML diagram frame

Every SysML diagram must have a frame. The frame represents a model element that provides the context for the diagram content. The diagram frame is a rectangle with a header, or label, containing standard information in the top left corner of the diagram. The rest of the area enclosed by the diagram frame is the content area, or canvas, where the symbols representing diagram content are drawn. An optional diagram description, providing further detail on the status and purpose of the diagram, can be attached to the frame boundary.

A frame defines the following information:

  • Diagram kind: an abbreviation indicating the kind of diagram

  • Model element type: the type of model element that the diagram frame represents

  • Model element name: the name of the represented model element

  • Diagram name: the name of the diagram, which is often used to say something about the diagram purpose

  • Diagram usage: a keyword indicating a specialized use of a diagram

SysML Requirement Diagram

A Requirements Diagram graphically shows the:

  • relationship among textual requirement elements <>, containment

  • relationship between requirements and model elements <>, <>

  • dependency between a requirement and a test case that verifies that the requirement is met <>.

SysML Block Definition Diagram (BDD)

A Block Definition Diagram graphically shows the basic structural elements (blocks) the system and their relationships / dependencies. Internal connectors are not shown.

The multiplicity is the definition of a cardinality - i.e., number of elements - of some collection of elements by providing a lower bound (possibly infinite) and a upper bound (possibly infinite too) that serve to provide an inclusive interval of non-negative integers to specify the allowable number of instances of described element.

SysML Internal BLock Diagram (IBD)

An Internal Block Diagram shows the realization of the system structure defined in the Block Definition Diagram. It is composed of a set of nested parts (i.e. instances of the system blocks) that are interconnected via ports and connectors.

 

SysML Use Case Diagram

A Use Case Diagram captures the services (external function) of a system by describing interactions between users of the system and the system itself. Note that as a system is decomposed, users of a given system could be external people or other systems. A use case diagram comprises a system boundary that contains a set of use cases. Actors lie outside of the system boundary and are bound to use cases via associations. A use case does not reveal or imply the system’s internal structure (“black-box” view).

SysML Activity Diagram

An Activity Diagram is similar to the classic flow chart. It describes a workflow, business process, or algorithm by decomposing the flow of execution into a set of actions and sub activities joined by transitions and various connectors. An activity diagram can be a simple linear sequence of actions or it can be a complex series of parallel actions with conditional branching and concurrency.

SysML State Machine Diagram (STM)

A State Machine Diagram describes the state-based behaviour of a block. It is sometimes considered as the most important behaviour diagram, as it aggregates the information from both the activity diagram (functional flow) and the sequence diagrams (interactions with the environment), and adds to it the event- driven block diagram. Basically, a statechart diagram is composed of a set of states joined by transitions and various connectors. An event may trigger a transition from one state to another. Actions can be performed on transitions and on state entry/exit.

SysML Sequence Diagram (STM)

Sequence Diagrams elaborate on requirements specified in use cases and activity diagrams by showing how actors and blocks collaborate in some behavior. A sequence diagram represents one or more scenarios through a use case. A sequence diagram is composed of vertical lifelines for the actors and blocks along with an ordered set of messages passed between these entities over a period of time.

SysML Parametric Diagram

A Parametric Diagram is a special type of an Internal Block Diagram. It visualizes the parametric relationship between system properties. It is an integral part of technical performance measures and trade studies.

Limits of Systems Engineering Modelling Languages

Most efforts to define new graphical conceptual modelling notations are spent on designing semantics while ignoring or undervaluing the role of visual syntax [Moody, 2009]. The lack of theoretical rigour when designing visual dialects lead to undesired properties – e.g.

  • lack of semiotic clarity due to numerous instances of symbol redundancy or overload,

  • very poor discriminability of conceptual modelling notations that consist of shapes – mostly rectangle variants [Petre, 1995] – and connecting lines that are visually too similar [Moody, 2009] therefore leading to confusion [Patig, 1999].

The poor quality of graphical conceptual modelling notations harms the acceptance from the non-specialist audience [McLeod 2018; Moody, 2009].

 

“Practitioners spend inordinate effort transposing models from rigorous tools to non-structured formats to overcome the acceptance problem. In the process, the connection to the source repository is lost, thereby destroying integrity, reusability, maintainability and currency of the derived output when the source changes**”** [McLeod, 2018].

Case Study 1 'B2B Large Scale System without Innovation': Helicopter System

In this example, the system-of-interest is an helicopter. The system architecture will be designed according to a specific systems engineering process and modelling method detailed hereunder and the modelling language - i.e., a collection of symbols, each having a shared conceptualization amongst the members of a domain of discourse - used to encode the concepts is SysML V1.

Develop System Requirements

Define System Life Cycle

The first main activity of the proposed Systems Engineering process is the definition of the problem space, that is, the definition of the system requirements (remember that the problem space can be seen as a vectorial space where each vector is a requirement). An helicopter system is a Brownfield/Legacy system. In such a legacy and large scale Business to Business (B2B) project, marketing does not play a key role in the definition of stakeholder needs and system requirements. This does not mean that there is no marketing needs expected by airline companies, but system requirements mainly result from the analysis of the system in its contexts of development, production, use, maintenance...

If you cannot collect the Voice Of Customers (in this case study, customers include system acquires, internal stakeholders, end-users, regulatory bodies, etc.), you can still start a needs elicitation process There is no single recipe for needs elicitation, it also depends on the preferred systems engineering process. In this case study, we will assume that to get a complete and correct set of requirements, we must start by identifying all potential sources of requirements, that is, the stakeholders and external systems. To avoid missing any stakeholder or external system, we will systematically define the system contexts for each life cycle phase.

Below is a definition of the Helicopter System Life Cycle using a SysML State Machine (stm) diagram. States, which are graphically represented by boxes, encode life cycle phases. Transitions between states are graphically represented by arrows and are used to encode the temporal sequence of life cycle phases. The Initial node represented by a black circle encodes the beginning of the system life cycle. The Final State node represented by a black circle within a white circle symbolizes the end of the helicopter life cycle.

For the rest of this tutorial, we will concentrate on the life cycle phase "Cruising" but the same Systems Engineering activities must be performed for every context of each life cycle phase.

Define System Contexts

Before read the section Develop System Requirements of the fundamentals of systems engineering.

The next activity consist in the definition of the H/C contexts, that is:

  • the identification of the external entities - stakeholders and external systems - that directly interact with the H/C or indirectly influence it, and

  • the environment conditions, that is, the properties of the operational environment within which the system will operate.

In each life cycle phase, the H/C can be part of one or multiple contexts. For instance, the life cycle phase "Landing" can be detailed by five contexts:

  • To land on clear area,

  • To land on heli-deck, oil rig, on slop, on ground helipad,

  • To land rolling,

  • To land for rolling test, and

  • To land on ship deck

Each context may include different external entities and environmental conditions and each external entity may expect specific system functions or impose design constraints according to the context. For instance, in the contexts "To land rolling" or "To land for rolling test", after touching down, the pilot will desire "To slow down" and "To control the trajectory on ground" (with differential braking). However, the system functions "To slow down" and "To control the trajectory on ground" would not be necessary part of the context "To land on heli-deck, oil rig, on slop, on ground helipad" since, in this life situation, the pilot will not expect to move on ground.

The graphical representation of a context diagram is a SysML Block Definition Diagram (BDD) where a block encoding the context is composed of the system of interest, stakholders, external systems and environmental conditions. Note that all these modelling elements are not part of the basic SysML vocabulary but are customized (with SysML stereotypes) SysML blocks to encode fundamental Systems Engineering concepts.

Populating your model will lead to the creation of thousands of modelling elements that must be logically organized. For example, to store the definition of a context, you may create four Packages to store the diagrams, the blocks encoding the contexts (e.g., landing context, cruising context...), the stereotyped blocks encoding the external entities and environmental conditions, and the block symbolizing the system of interest.

After creating a BDD for capturing the definition of a system context, you can create a traceability relationship by simply dragging and dropping the context diagram on the life cycle phase. A BDD symbol will be attached to the state of the state machine diagram .

Define System Effect-Functions

For the Cruising context we may ask to the stakeholders what services the Helicopter System must provide to them. For instance, knowing that the legacy (or brownfield) Helicopter System has a landing gear subystem, the pilot desires to perform 4 main functions while cruising:

  • To lock the wheel of the nose landing gear in axis (to avoid structural damages due to collision with the fuselage while retracting),

  • To extend the landing gear in nominal mode,

  • To retract the landing gear, and

  • To extend the landing gear in emergency mode (i.e., when system is encountering a failure).

Functions must be implementation-free!!! We usually pretend that the functions performed by (aka. or services provided by) a system must be solution-agnostic, that is, free of any design and/or implementation choices. So, why the landing gear should be retractable and equipped with wheels? Why don't we prefer skids ?! More radically, in a greenfield/clean sheet development, we may ignore the landing gear subsystem and ask: what are the "right" services (aka. system functions) the helicopter has to provide (aka. perform) to the test pilot? One technique to identify those solution-free functions is to challenge the design choice by answering the question "Why do we need a landing gear?". Two main system functions 1/ To minimize fuel consumption (by reducing aerodynamic loads) and 2/ To minimize noise (inside and outside) would motivate the choice for a retractable landing gear, whereas other main function 3/ To move on ground requires wheels rather than skids. When developing such legacy systems, the main objective is not to foster creativity and innovation but to master the system complexity. Therefore, it is not necessary to ignore the existing design but you should always challenge paraphrases of hasty design decisions by asking "Why?" and find the right compromise.
